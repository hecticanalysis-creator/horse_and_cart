      ▸ How Victorian Fuel Pricing Works
      The Weekly Price Cycle

      Victoria's fuel market follows a predictable weekly cycle driven by coordinated retailer behaviour. Prices hit a low point on Sunday or Monday before jumping sharply — often 10–25¢ per litre — on Tuesday or Wednesday morning. They then gradually drift back down over the following days.

      This cycle is not random. The ACCC (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission) has documented the cycle extensively and attributes it to market signalling between major retailers, particularly the way one brand's price rise is quickly matched by competitors.

      Trough days: Sunday–Monday. Peak: Tuesday–Wednesday. The cycle repeats approximately every 7–10 days. Metro Melbourne follows the cycle most sharply; regional Victoria follows with a 1–2 day lag.
      What Drives the Base Price

      Victorian retail fuel prices ultimately reflect global crude oil markets, primarily Brent Crude and Singapore Mogas 95 (the regional benchmark). The supply chain is:

      Crude oil → refined overseas (mainly Singapore, South Korea) → shipped to Australia → terminal storage → wholesale → retailer → pump.

      The Australian dollar exchange rate against the US dollar has a significant impact — a weaker AUD means higher import costs. Singapore Mogas is priced in USD, so AUD/USD movements flow directly through to the pump within 2–4 weeks.

      Rule of thumb: Each US$10/barrel move in crude oil translates to roughly 2–3¢/litre change at the pump, delayed by 2–6 weeks.

      Pricing Transparency & Regulation

      Victoria operates a mandatory fuel price reporting scheme under the Fuel Prices Act 2021. All fuel retailers must report prices to the government within one hour of any price change. This data is made publicly available through the Servo Saver API (fuel.service.vic.gov.au), which powers this dashboard.

      The scheme — administered by Consumer Affairs Victoria — was introduced specifically to increase transparency and help consumers find cheaper fuel. Victoria is one of only a few Australian states with mandatory real-time reporting.

      Western Australia has operated FuelWatch since 2000, where retailers must set their price for the next day by 2pm — providing 24 hours of price certainty. Victoria's scheme is real-time rather than advance-notice.
      ▸ Complete Fuel Types Guide
      Victoria's service stations offer up to 11 fuel types. Understanding the differences can save you money and protect your engine. Always check your vehicle owner's manual before switching fuels.
      U91
      Unleaded 91
      RON (octane rating): 91
      The standard unleaded petrol. Suitable for most petrol engines designed for 91 RON fuel. Most affordable of the petrol grades.

      Who should use it: Vehicles whose owner's manual specifies "regular unleaded" or "minimum 91 RON". The majority of Australian passenger cars.

      Caution: Don't use if your car requires 95 or 98 RON minimum — engine knock can cause damage over time.
      E10
      Ethanol 10% Blend
      RON: 94 effective
      Regular unleaded blended with 10% ethanol. Typically 4–8¢/L cheaper than U91. Slightly higher octane than U91 due to the ethanol.

      Who should use it: Most post-2000 vehicles. Approved for use in ~95% of modern petrol cars. Check your manual — look for "E10 compatible" or "ethanol up to 10%".

      Important: Not suitable for some older vehicles, motorcycles, or small engines (mowers, boats). Ethanol attracts moisture — avoid in vehicles stored long-term.
      P95
      Premium 95
      RON: 95 minimum
      Mid-grade premium petrol. Suitable for engines requiring at least 95 RON. Typically 15–20¢/L more than U91.

      Who should use it: Vehicles whose manual specifies "premium 95" or "minimum 95 RON" — common in European and some Japanese performance vehicles.

      Myth busted: Putting 95 or 98 in a car that only requires 91 provides no benefit — your ECU isn't tuned to take advantage of the higher octane.
      P98
      Premium 98
      RON: 98 minimum
      The highest-octane petrol widely available in Australia. Typically 25–35¢/L above U91. Often contains additional engine cleaning additives.

      Who should use it: High-performance and turbocharged engines requiring 98 RON minimum — certain BMW, Mercedes, Porsche, and high-spec Japanese sports cars.

      Performance note: In a car tuned for 98 RON, the ECU can advance ignition timing and extract more power and efficiency. The premium may be partially offset by improved fuel economy.
      DSL
      Diesel
      Cetane rating: 51 minimum (Australian standard)
      Standard diesel for compression-ignition engines. Used in most trucks, vans, SUVs, and diesel passenger cars. Currently the most expensive common fuel in Victoria.

      Diesel vs petrol: Diesel engines are typically 25–40% more fuel-efficient by energy than equivalent petrol engines — the higher per-litre cost is partly offset by lower consumption.

      Warning: Misfuelling — putting petrol in a diesel — can cause serious engine damage. The filler nozzle for petrol is smaller; most modern diesels have a misfuel prevention device.
      PDSL
      Premium Diesel
      Higher cetane, additional additives
      Enhanced diesel with cetane improvers, detergents, and lubricity additives. Brands include BP Ultimate Diesel, Shell V-Power Diesel.

      Who benefits: High-performance diesel engines, particularly European vehicles (BMW, Mercedes, VW/Audi TDI). Regular diesel engines see minimal benefit.

      Cost: Usually 5–15¢/L above standard diesel. Can be worth it for vehicles with tight fuel injector tolerances that benefit from the cleaning additives.
      E85
      Ethanol 85% Blend
      RON: ~105 equivalent
      High-ethanol flex-fuel containing 70–85% ethanol and 15–30% petrol. Very high octane. Only available at selected servos in Victoria.

      Who can use it: Flex-fuel vehicles (FFV) only — requires special fuel lines, injectors, and ECU tuning. Do not use in a standard petrol engine.

      Economy note: Ethanol has ~34% less energy per litre than petrol, so fuel consumption increases by ~25–30% on E85. Check that the price saving justifies the consumption increase.
      LPG
      Liquefied Petroleum Gas
      Propane/butane mixture
      LPG (also called Autogas) is significantly cheaper per litre — typically 85–100¢/L vs 250–260¢ for ULP. However, LPG has lower energy density, so consumption is ~25% higher.

      Who uses it: Dedicated or dual-fuel LPG vehicles. Taxis historically used LPG extensively. Availability has declined significantly as the LPG vehicle fleet ages out.

      Converting: Aftermarket LPG conversions are possible but expensive ($2,000–4,000+). Payback period has lengthened as the LPG price advantage has narrowed.
      B20
      Biodiesel 20% Blend
      20% biodiesel, 80% mineral diesel
      Biodiesel is made from vegetable oils or animal fats. B20 is the most common biodiesel blend in Australia — high enough for measurable emissions benefits, low enough for broad compatibility.

      Benefits: Reduced particulate matter and lifecycle CO₂ emissions compared to mineral diesel. Better lubricity can extend injector life.

      Caution: Some manufacturers void warranties above B5. Check OEM guidelines. B20 can degrade rubber seals in older engines and may gel in cold weather.

      Federal Fuel Excise

      The fuel excise tax is the single largest fixed component of the petrol price. As of early 2026, it sits at approximately 49.6¢ per litre for petrol and diesel.

      The excise is indexed to CPI and adjusted twice yearly (August and February). It applies uniformly to all petrol, diesel, and blended fuels sold in Australia.

      During the 2022 cost-of-living crisis, the federal government temporarily halved the excise to ~22¢/L for 6 months, saving consumers roughly 11¢/L at the pump. The measure expired in September 2022.

      GST on excise: GST is applied to the excise-inclusive price, so you pay GST on the excise — a "tax on a tax". This adds roughly 5¢/L to the effective tax burden.

      The excise collected from road transport fuels contributes approximately $19–22 billion per year to federal revenue, but is not hypothecated specifically to road funding.

      ▸ Victoria's Fuel Market — Structure & Players
      Market Structure

      Victoria has approximately 2,200–2,500 service stations, making it the second-largest state fuel market after NSW. The market is dominated by a small number of major brands but features a surprisingly large independent segment.

      SegmentEst. Market ShareKey Players
      Major integrated~55%BP, Shell/Viva, Ampol/Caltex
      Supermarket-linked~20%Coles Express (EG Group), Woolworths/EG
      Discount/wholesale~5%Costco, United
      Independent~20%Liberty, Apco, Metro, local operators
      Why Independents Are Often Cheaper

      Independent fuel retailers typically have lower overhead costs — no franchise fees, more flexible sourcing, and often located in lower-rent areas. They purchase fuel at terminal gate prices and can pass savings on.

      Major branded stations pay for the brand, loyalty program integration, and consistent supply — costs that are built into their pricing. The ACCC has consistently found that unbranded and independent stations average 2–6¢/L less than majors for equivalent fuel grades.

      Costco is consistently among the cheapest in Victoria due to its loss-leader fuel pricing model — fuel drives store membership and foot traffic rather than needing to be profitable on its own.

      ▸ Smart Fuelling — Detailed Consumer Guide
      E10 — The Smart Switch

      Switching from U91 to E10 is one of the easiest ways to cut your fuel bill. At a 6¢/L saving and 60L tank, that's $3.60 per fill, or ~$187/year at weekly fills.

      The consumption penalty is small: Ethanol has about 34% less energy than petrol, but E10 only contains 10% ethanol. In practice, the fuel economy difference is 1–3% — almost imperceptible in real-world driving. The price saving far exceeds the consumption penalty.

      Is your car compatible? Check the inside of your fuel cap door or owner's manual for "E10" or "ethanol up to 10%". The FCAI (Federal Chamber of Automotive Industries) maintains a compatibility checker at fcai.com.au.

      Not compatible: Vehicles manufactured before ~1986, some motorcycles, marine engines, older Mazda RX-series rotary engines, and some classic cars with older fuel system materials.

      Driving Habits That Actually Reduce Fuel Use
      • 1Smooth acceleration and braking — aggressive driving increases fuel consumption by 20–40%. Anticipate traffic and coast where possible.
      • 2Speed matters: Aerodynamic drag increases with the square of speed. Driving at 110km/h uses roughly 25% more fuel than 90km/h.
      • 3Tyre pressure: Under-inflated tyres increase rolling resistance. Keep tyres at the manufacturer's recommended pressure — check monthly.
      • 4Air conditioning: A/C can increase fuel consumption by 10–25% in city driving. Use windows at low speeds and A/C on the highway where open windows create drag.
      • 5Roof racks and carriers: An empty roof rack increases drag by ~5%. Remove them when not in use.
      • 6Engine warm-up: Modern fuel-injected engines don't need idling warm-up. Drive gently for the first few minutes instead.

      Data Sources

      Live price data: Servo Saver Open Data API (fuel.service.vic.gov.au), operated by Service Victoria. Updated in near real-time as retailers submit price changes.

      Mandatory reporting: Under the Fuel Prices Act 2021 (Vic), all fuel retailers must report price changes within one hour. This makes Victoria's data uniquely comprehensive.

      A personal reference dashboard for Victorian fuel prices. Built as a static HTML file generated by a local Python script and hosted on GitHub Pages.
